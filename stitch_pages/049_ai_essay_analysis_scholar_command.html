Navigating the Intersection of Architecture and Social Equity

My fascination with the built environment began not in a museum or a cathedral, but in the cramped, sun-drenched alleyways of my childhood neighborhood. I watched as the architecture defined the social interactions of the residents. The narrow passages dictated a forced intimacy, a shared existence that flourished despite the physical constraints.

As I transitioned into my formal studies, I began to realize that architecture is never just about aesthetics or structural integrity. It is a political statement. The unforgiving glass towers of the financial district stand in stark contrast to the dilapidated community centers of the suburbs. This disparity ignited a passion for what I call "Equity by Design."

I believe that the role of the modern architect is to serve as a bridge between the historical context of a site and the future needs of its marginalized inhabitants. In my senior capstone project, I proposed a modular housing system that prioritizes community space over individual footprint. This was not just a design challenge; it was an exercise in empathy.

The challenge of the 21st century is not building higher, but building wider—expanding our reach to those who have been systematically excluded from the benefits of thoughtful urban planning. I seek to join the Master of Architecture program at Yale because I believe its commitment to social impact aligns with my vision of a more inclusive world.
