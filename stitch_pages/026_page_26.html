The scent of burning sage always brings me back to my grandmother’s kitchen in Oaxaca, where the line between the physical and the spiritual felt as thin as a handmade tortilla. It was here that I first learned that leadership wasn't about the loudest voice in the room, but about the hands that prepared the meal.

As a child, I watched Abuela lead our community not through titles or decrees, but through the quiet orchestration of our neighborhood's mutual aid network. When the rainy season was late, she didn't organize a protest; she organized a seed bank. This pragmatic approach to communal survival became the blueprint for my own activism at Southside High.

The challenge was encountered by me during my junior year when our school's arts funding was slashed. Remembering Abuela's kitchen, I realized that we didn't need a savior; we needed a system.

I spent months collaborating with local muralsists and business owners to create 'The Living Canvas'—a rotating street gallery that funded our after-school programs through small-business sponsorships. This project wasn't just about art; it was about reclaiming the agency that Abuela had modeled.

In the face of adversity, we often look for heroes. But my journey has taught me that true impact lies in the "hand-to-hand" work of building sustainable community infrastructure. I am applying to your university not just to study social policy, but to refine the tools I've already begun to build.
